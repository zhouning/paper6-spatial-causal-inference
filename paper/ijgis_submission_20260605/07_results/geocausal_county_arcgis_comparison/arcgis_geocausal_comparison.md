## Main conclusion

GeoCausal reproduces the central ArcGIS conclusion: counties with higher social
capital have higher expected average age at death after adjusting for health,
socioeconomic, behavioral, environmental, and spatial-context variables.

The strongest agreement is in the descriptive association: ArcGIS reports
`R^2 = 0.37`, while GeoCausal's exposure-only model gives `R^2 = 0.3719`.
The adjusted causal direction is also consistent: GeoCausal estimates a positive
effect of `0.1465` years per one-unit increase in social capital, with a grouped
bootstrap interval that remains positive.

The main difference is the exact way shape is represented. ArcGIS emphasizes a
steeper ERF segment after social capital around `13`. GeoCausal's gridded ERF is
smooth and monotonic, but the ArcGIS-compatible trimmed sample shows a statistically
clear piecewise slope increase after `13`. For Paper 6, the defensible wording is
therefore:

> The open-source GeoCausal SCCA framework independently reproduces the positive
> direction and similar magnitude of the ArcGIS causal-inference result. Under the
> same exposure-tail trimming rule used by ArcGIS, GeoCausal also supports a
> steeper social-capital/longevity slope beyond about `13`, although its gridded
> ERF remains smoother than the ArcGIS visualization.

## Recommended next experiment

To make the comparison more exact, add an ArcGIS-compatible analysis mode to
GeoCausal:

1. Add generalized propensity score matching or IPW balance diagnostics that report
   an ArcGIS-like weighted average balance metric.
2. Refit the ERF with a nonlinear smoother or spline so threshold behavior near
   exposure `13` can be tested rather than assumed.
3. Add a target-exposure solver for questions such as the ArcGIS example
   `4.6 -> 34.6` for a target average death age of `70`.
4. Add ArcGIS-style local/target ERF outputs so county-specific target narratives
   can be reproduced directly by the open-source pipeline.
